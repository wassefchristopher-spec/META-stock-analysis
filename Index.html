Business Model & Moat

How Meta Makes Money

Meta's business is almost entirely advertising — about 98% of revenue comes from selling ad space across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, and Threads. The product in plain English: nearly 4 billion people use Meta's apps to scroll, message, and share, and Meta's AI algorithms watch what each person engages with, then sell businesses the ability to show that exact person a relevant ad at exactly the right moment. The better the AI gets at predicting what you'll click on, the more advertisers are willing to pay per ad — which is why ad prices rose 12% even as the number of ads shown only grew 19%. Reality Labs (VR/AR headsets, smart glasses) is the side bet — currently a money-losing division building toward Meta's longer-term vision of AI-powered wearable computing.

Moat Analysis

Meta's moat is built on scale and data, reinforced by network effects:

  • Close to 4 billion monthly active users across the app family — unmatched scale in social
  • Network effects: your friends and family are already on these apps, hard to replicate
  • Decades of behavioral ad-targeting data feeding increasingly sophisticated AI models
  • Custom MTIA silicon (in-house chip program) reduces dependence on NVIDIA, captures margin
  • Open-source Llama strategy builds a developer ecosystem that lowers Meta's own AI costs
  • Vertical integration across the entire ad stack — no other platform sees this much user signal

Top 3 Competitors

Alphabet Search + YouTube
Amazon Retail media + AWS
TikTok/ByteDance Short-form video

Alphabet competes directly for global digital ad dollars but through a different intent layer (search vs. social discovery). Amazon's retail media business is growing fast and benefits from purchase-intent data Meta doesn't have. TikTok is the most direct threat to engagement time, particularly among younger users, though Meta's Reels has closed much of that gap — Reels now accounts for more than half of time spent on Instagram.

  • Planned workforce optimization announced — watch for margin impact in coming quarters
  • Threads and WhatsApp monetization — still underexploited relative to Facebook/Instagram
Asymmetry & Deals

Asymmetry Check

The asymmetry here is genuinely favorable relative to its mega-cap AI peers. The floor is unusually solid for a stock of this size — a real, profitable, cash-generative advertising business with 41% operating margins and a 20.5x trailing P/E, among the cheapest of the large AI-exposed names. The ceiling is open: if AI-enhanced ad targeting keeps improving monetization per user the way it has, and if Reality Labs eventually breaks even, there's real upside beyond just "the ad business grows with the economy." The main risk isn't valuation — it's execution risk on a capex budget that just got raised to as much as $145 billion, with no guarantee that spending translates one-for-one into ad revenue growth.

Government & Major Partnerships

  • Nebius — up to $27B multi-year AI infrastructure deal; $12B committed, Vera Rubin GPU platform
  • Google Cloud — ~$10B cloud capacity contract, diversifying compute supply beyond AWS/Azure
  • NVIDIA — major GPU purchaser; also using AMD chips alongside new NVIDIA systems
  • Broadcom — custom MTIA silicon partner; multiple gigawatts of XPUs planned from 2027
  • Crusoe — new deal signed to secure additional AI computing power (June 2026)
  • $107B step-up in contractual commitments in Q1 2026 alone from multi-year cloud/infra deals
  • No direct US government contract like Cameco's, but indirectly tied to US AI policy and export rules on chips
Bull vs Bear

🐂 Bull Case

Meta is quietly the cheapest of the major AI-exposed mega-caps while putting up the best growth numbers among them — revenue just grew at its fastest pace since 2021, and that's before the bulk of its AI investments have even started monetizing. The advertising machine keeps getting better, not worse, with AI improving both how many ads it can show and how much advertisers are willing to pay for them. Unlike pure infrastructure plays, Meta doesn't need to convince the world that AI demand is real — it already has 4 billion people generating engagement data every day, and AI just makes the existing business more efficient. If even part of Reality Labs and AI glasses eventually pays off, that's a free option layered on top of an already excellent core business trading at a reasonable multiple.

🐻 Bear Case

The capex number is the story to watch — Meta just raised its 2026 spending guide to as much as $145 billion, and a lot of that money is going into compute that may or may not generate a proportional return. If AI infrastructure spending across the entire industry turns out to be ahead of actual AI revenue, Meta is one of the biggest spenders in that bubble, not a bystander. User growth already stumbled this quarter, missing estimates, and even if the explanation given was one-off geopolitical disruptions, any further softening would be a bad sign for a stock priced on continued engagement growth. Regulatory risk in the EU remains a real, recurring threat to the ad-targeting model the entire business depends on, and youth-related litigation continues to be an overhang that could result in costly settlements down the line.

Analyst Summary

Short Analysis

Meta stands out among the mega-cap AI names for a simple reason: the fundamentals have actually caught up to — and arguably outrun — the stock price. Q1 2026 revenue grew 33% year-over-year, the fastest pace since 2021, with a 41% operating margin that very few companies at this scale can match. Trading at a 20.5x trailing P/E and a forward P/E around 22x, Meta is priced more like a steady industrial than a company growing earnings 28%+ this year — that gap is the core of the bull case.

Nearly every metric on this checklist lands inside or above the ideal range: gross margins near 81%, EBITDA margins near 48%, a PEG ratio around 1.4 that's still inside the "fairly valued" band, and an EV/EBITDA multiple of roughly 15-17x that's actually cheaper than several AI infrastructure peers despite Meta owning the demand side of that infrastructure, not just the supply side. The one real soft spot was daily active people missing estimates this quarter, though management attributed that to one-off disruptions in Iran and Russia rather than a structural slowdown.

The honest risk isn't valuation, it's capital discipline. Meta just raised its 2026 capex guidance to as much as $145 billion — nearly double what it spent in 2025 — and the market will eventually demand to see that translate into either revenue growth or margin expansion. So far the company has delivered on that promise quarter after quarter, which is exactly why this name screens as a genuine large-cap anchor rather than a speculative AI bet: real earnings, real cash flow, real moat, at a price that doesn't require you to assume everything goes perfectly.
